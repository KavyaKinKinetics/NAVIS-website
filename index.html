The Challenge

Orbital congestion is now a governance problem

Operators have conjunction alerts and tracking systems. What they lack is a structured, quantitative framework for determining when a satellite should exit orbit.

End-of-life decisions are made today across disconnected silos — with no unified model to arbitrate the trade-offs between engineering, compliance, and economic risk.

I
Engineering
Monitors fuel and subsystem health — but ignores economic value and regulatory timing constraints.
II
Compliance
Tracks static rules (the 25-year rule, now 5-year) without dynamic stochastic risk assessment.
III
Finance
Models asset depreciation but lacks orbital context for risk-adjusted exposure quantification.
Result: disconnected silos. No unified model. Suboptimal disposal timing and compounding liability risk.
The Framework

Five layers.
One auditable output.

A deterministic decision architecture. Each layer contributes a quantified signal to the final recommendation.

Layer 01
Satellite State Data
Telemetry, fuel reserves, battery health, mission age, and orbital parameters from the satellite bus.
Layer 02
Regulatory Compliance Gates
IADC thresholds and evolving mandates (5-year rule) encoded as live decision logic, not static checklists.
Layer 03
Disposal Success Probability
Reliability modelling of post-mission disposal maneuvers from health signals and orbital decay trends.
Layer 04
Stochastic Collision Risk
Poisson encounter modelling across altitude shells, exposure duration, and orbital density — quantifying systemic cascade risk.
Layer 05
Decision & Economic Exposure
All signals synthesised into a scored, explainable recommendation with audit trail, insurance classification, and operator actions.
Differentiation
